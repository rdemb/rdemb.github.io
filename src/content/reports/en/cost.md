## Summary

Costco is a company the entire council regards as exceptional, and that is exactly why it is a trap. The membership model produces a repeatable, recession-resistant stream of cash, ROE runs at 24 percent, and member retention has historically exceeded 90 percent. The problem is not the company, it is the price. At a P/E of 53, a P/B of 12.9, and a PEG of 5.3, the market has priced in perfection with no margin of safety whatsoever. Six of the seven council members say "watch" or "avoid," and only one is willing to say "hold." The committee's verdict: wait for a better price, because you buy a good company on good terms or not at all.

The figures in this report come from SEC filings (10-K/10-Q): diluted EPS of $18.21, revenue growing 8.2 percent year over year, EPS up 10 percent, and a dividend yield of 0.55 percent. This is the real pace of a mature compounder, not an extrapolation of a one-off jump, and it is precisely what shows the scale of the overpayment at a P/E of 53.

## Business and Moat

Costco's moat is behavioral rather than technological. The real profit comes from membership fees, which are almost pure margin, not from selling merchandise. The thin 2.9 percent net margin and 3.8 percent operating margin are a deliberate part of the design: low prices drive traffic, traffic drives memberships, and membership fees fund the low prices. It is a self-reinforcing machine, and Amazon cannot clone the ritual of the warehouse and the price per unit. A low beta of 0.87 confirms the defensive character.

This is also why classic value metrics mislead here. A P/B of 12.9 and a 2.9 percent net margin look terrible through Graham's lens, but for a membership model that deliberately keeps assets low, they are the wrong yardsticks. The balance sheet is healthy: $18.9 billion in cash is more than triple the $5.7 billion of debt, and debt to equity sits at 17 percent, so the company carries no net leverage. A total loss is practically impossible.

## Valuation and Scenarios

This is where the whole argument sits. Free cash flow on the order of $7.8 billion against a $431 billion market cap implies a free cash flow yield of about 1.8 percent. You are paying today, up front, for many years of flawless growth, while the real pace in the filings is 8 percent revenue and 10 percent EPS. The analyst target of $1,082 is barely 11 percent above the current price, a weak reward for the risk taken.

The optimistic scenario: comparable sales keep growing solidly, executive membership penetration approaches half the base, a fee increase comes through, and the market keeps paying 53 times earnings for the only defensive compounder with retention like this. In that world, waiting for a pullback is the classic mistake of missing out, because Costco has looked expensive for a decade and has never disappointed.

The cautious scenario: earnings growth stays at its current single-digit pace, and the multiple returns to its decade-long median around 30 to 38 times. The de-rating alone then eats years of growth and produces a drawdown on the order of 30 to 40 percent, even though the business is performing beautifully. This is not bankruptcy risk, it is the risk of the price paid. Anyone who bought at 970 could wait several years just to break even, exactly as Walmart holders did from 2000 to 2012.

## Red Team Test

The Red Team attacked the thesis from both sides. From the bull side: Costco is a compounder that always looks expensive, and waiting for a P/E of 30 may be waiting for Godot, since the stock rose sixfold over a decade despite an "overly expensive" price. From the bear side: multiple compression plus growth normalization is a real scenario, not a theoretical one, and a great company bought at too high a multiple can deliver zero return over ten years.

The hardest substantive blow: a P/E of 53 against 10 percent EPS growth yields a PEG of 5.3, so the multiple discounts a pace three to four times higher than the actual one. To justify today's price, Costco would have to either hold a 53-times valuation for the entire decade or double its growth rate, and neither assumption is supported by the filings. The team's verdict: the recommendation survived the attack. We are not changing direction, we openly acknowledge the risk of missing out, but the math of the multiple against growth stays on the side of caution.

## Risk and Position Sizing

The main risk is a permanent loss of capital through the premium paid, not through the company's collapse. At the current valuation, any disappointment in the growth rate hits the stock harder than any operational weakness would. Secondarily: erosion of the price advantage, if automation of rivals' logistics levels their unit cost with Costco's scale effect.

Under OBSERVE-ONLY logic, the council does not open a position. If the CEO wanted exposure despite the valuation, the only acceptable form would be a small position, built in tranches, reaching full size only on a meaningful compression of the multiple. A sensible threshold to watch is a P/E below about 35 times, or genuine market pessimism, for example a renewal rate dropping below 89 percent or comparable sales below 4 percent.

## Committee Verdict

Watch. Costco is a business you want to own, bought at a price you do not want to own. The distribution of signals is unambiguous: five "watch" votes, one "hold," one "avoid," and zero "buy." The committee will not fake a consensus around buying, because there is none. We wait for a better price and monitor the real data from the filings (whether revenue and EPS growth hold, the multiple's trend relative to the decade-long median, the renewal rate). The position stays on the watchlist, not in the portfolio.

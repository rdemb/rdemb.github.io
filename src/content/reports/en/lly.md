## Summary

Eli Lilly is a company the entire council regards as exceptional, and the key change from the first look is this: the high price here has real backing in growth. Tirzepatide (Mounjaro in diabetes, Zepbound in obesity) is a drug with a real clinical edge, and Lilly together with Novo Nordisk forms an effective duopoly in the category that defines the whole decade in pharma. The figures from the SEC filings are outstanding: revenue is growing 44.7 percent year over year, EPS nearly doubled (+96 percent), net income rose 94.9 percent, the net margin is 31.7 percent, and return on equity is 66.2 percent.

The heart of the matter is the valuation seen through the right multiple. A P/E of 49.3, a P/S of 15.5 and a P/B of 32.3 sound high until you set them against the pace of growth. A PEG of 0.51 says something simple: the market is paying less than half of what is usually considered a fair price for a year of earnings growth. That reverses the original thesis. Lilly is not an expensive defensive compounder priced for perfection. It is a company in a hypergrowth phase whose price is, for now, justified by the growth itself. The whole axis of the debate shifts from "is it too expensive" to "is this pace of growth durable".

The council's distribution now tilts toward cautious accumulation: three say accumulate, two say light accumulation or watch, two stay at watch, and nobody says avoid. The dispute is not about the quality of the business or even the valuation itself, it is about the durability of the growth anomaly. The committee's verdict is to accumulate in small tranches, treating the durability of the GLP-1 boom as the main factor that could break the thesis.

## Business and moat

Lilly's moat is real and technically understandable. Tirzepatide is a dual GIP and GLP-1 agonist, clinically more effective at reducing body weight than a competitor's pure GLP-1. This is not a first-mover advantage, it is a better molecule backed by a next-generation pipeline: orforglipron (an oral, small-molecule drug that sidesteps the injection problem and scales like an ordinary pill) and retatrutide (a triple agonist). On top of that sits a manufacturing moat. Multibillion-dollar capital expenditure on peptide production capacity is in itself a barrier to entry that a new player cannot leap over in a year.

The signature of this moat shows up in profitability. A 31.7 percent net margin and a 66.2 percent return on equity are the mark of patents and intellectual property, not of a commodity. A low beta of 0.52 says this is a defensive healthcare name, not speculative technology. The obesity market is still on the steep part of the growth curve, with further indications on the way (sleep apnea, heart disease, fatty liver disease), and it is that curve that drives revenue growth measured in the tens of percent a year.

The warning appears around concentration. A large part of the growth thesis rests on a single mechanism. Mounjaro and Zepbound are the same molecule. One safety issue, one weaker comparative readout, or one drug-pricing decision from a regulator hits a meaningful part of the earnings engine at once.

## Valuation and scenarios

This is where the whole case plays out. The valuation looks threatening nominally until you weigh it by the pace of growth. A P/E of 49.3 with EPS growth of 96 percent gives a PEG of roughly 0.51. That means that even after a doubling of the stock in a year, the market still does not price Lilly as expensive relative to the dynamics the company actually shows. For a category leader in a market measured in the hundreds of billions of dollars, this is not absurd, it is a bull argument grounded in hard numbers from the filings rather than in narrative.

The other side of the equation is the balance sheet and the quality of the price itself. Equity is 31.2 billion against 40.4 billion of debt and 5.3 billion of cash, which gives a debt-to-equity ratio of 129 percent. A current ratio of 1.5 is healthy, but this is not a company with a net cash cushion. A P/B of 32.3 means that almost the entire valuation is expected future earnings, not assets on the balance sheet. In other words, growth defends the price, but there is no margin of safety on the asset side. If growth slows faster than the market assumes, the stock has nothing to lean on but the momentum itself.

Scenarios for the next 12 to 24 months:

- Bull: orforglipron and retatrutide post excellent data, the obesity market grows faster than consensus, and competitors fall behind on supply. Revenue growth stays high, the sub-1 PEG proves to be an accurate signal, and the stock keeps rising along with earnings.
- Base: growth decelerates from the mid-forties toward the twenties, margins drift down under pressure from competition and payers, and the multiple normalizes. The stock rises more slowly than earnings, but the growth thesis stays intact.
- Bear: a GLP-1 price war (Novo cuts prices, oral substitutes arrive, Medicare forces rebates) cuts the margin and the growth rate at the same time. The high P/E and P/B then lose their backing, and the stock loses 30 to 40 percent regardless of the company's quality.

## Red Team test

The Red Team attacked the thesis from both sides and confirmed its direction while making it more realistic.

On the too-easy-enthusiasm side: a PEG of 0.51 is real only as long as the growth rate in the denominator is real. EPS growth of 96 percent is a phenomenon of a new category's launch phase, not a norm you can extrapolate for years. As the comparison base grows, the math itself trims the dynamics, and then the PEG can quickly jump from 0.5 toward 2 or 3. The entire bull thesis stands on the durability of a single number.

On the too-mild-caution side: the previous look anchored on the nominal P/E and on data artifacts, and so it missed the most important fact. Anyone who said too expensive a year ago at 624 dollars missed a doubling of the stock precisely because the growth was real and underappreciated. A structural category leader is easy to miss when you anchor on the multiple alone without looking at what that multiple finances.

The Red Team's most important contribution is the data correction. The earlier version rested on diverging figures from the automated brief (among other things an inflated ROE and cash flows with no support in the filings). The clean SEC data gives a coherent picture: real, very high revenue and earnings growth, a high but justified multiple, and concrete balance-sheet risks (D/E of 129 percent, P/B of 32) and business risks (the durability of the boom). The Red Team's verdict: the direction is cautious accumulation, but keep the threshold for adding disciplined and watch the durability of growth, not the price alone.

## Risk and position sizing

The biggest risk is the durability of the growth rate. The whole thesis, including the favorable PEG, rests on the assumption that revenue and earnings dynamics persist longer. When GLP-1 moves from hypergrowth into maturity, the PEG denominator falls and the high multiple loses its justification. Add to that three concrete factors: Novo Nordisk's competition and pricing pressure (which can cut the margin), the concentration on a single mechanism, and a thin balance sheet with a high D/E of 129 percent and a P/B of 32, with no asset buffer.

In position-sizing terms this is a candidate for a position built gradually, not for a one-off purchase of full weight at the current price. Accumulating in small tranches lets you participate in the growth while spreading the risk that the GLP-1 boom loses pace faster than the market assumes. Adding is worth tying to evidence of durability: further quarters of revenue growth held clearly above 20 percent despite competitive entry, and good comparative data for the next generation of drugs.

What to monitor: quarterly revenue and earnings dynamics (whether the pace holds or slows), comparative data for orforglipron and retatrutide against the competitor, Novo's pricing moves and new entrants, the first quarter with guidance below expectations, U.S. drug-pricing policy, and the level of debt and net margin in the coming filings.

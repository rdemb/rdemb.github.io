I love Stanisław Lem. Not because he wrote about robots and space, though he did. Because he wrote about the limits of knowing, and he did it with a humility that is rare today.

Lem never promised that technology would solve everything. He showed how people, while building something smarter than themselves, keep falling into their own illusions. "Solaris" is not a story about an ocean. It is a story about standing in front of something we will not understand, and stubbornly forcing our human categories onto it.

I think about this when I work with AI. It is easy to believe a model "understands". Most of the time it simply guesses well, and we fill in the rest. Lem taught me to ask harder: how do I know this works, rather than just sounds good? I ask the same question of my trading strategies and my experiments. The difference between "sounds smart" and "works" is the whole craft.

That is why I keep coming back to Lem. Not for predictions, but for mental hygiene. For the reminder that humility toward what we do not know is not weakness. It is the only honest place to start.

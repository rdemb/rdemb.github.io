For two years I systematically searched the data for one thing: a repeatable, precise direction signal that an automated system could trade on its own. Candles and their patterns. Hidden market regimes. Order flow in the version available to retail. Interest rates, fund positioning, the dollar and yields over twenty years of history. Nine separate fronts, each with a placebo test, each on data the model had not seen before.

The result is monotonous: at the level available from a retail terminal, precise direction does not exist. The structure in the data is there, sometimes beautifully regular. The edge that pays for guessing the side of the market after costs is not. I could have been in denial, but the data was stubborn, and I would rather lose to the data than win against my own imagination.

## So what does the automated system do here

If the machine cannot pick direction, then why do I even need an EA? For what the machine does better than me. And it does three things better.

It measures. All the time, without boredom, without mood. What hour it is and how much the market usually gives at that time. How much of today's range budget is already used. How close to the extremes of the day the price sits and what the computed chance is that the day adds a new one. A human can check this once in a while. The machine never stops.

It watches. Position size comes from numbers, not from conviction. Before important data there is silence, and the system knows the calendar better than I do. A time of day with bad math is out of the game. The stop is where it is supposed to be, not where it hurts less.

It executes. This is the most important part. At 16:58, after two losses, with a position slightly underwater, a human starts negotiating with themselves. Just a little longer. Maybe it bounces. The machine does not negotiate. A rule that passed the test is carried out the same way on Monday morning and Friday evening.

Direction stays on my side. Reading the situation, the context of the day, the decision that today I trade and tomorrow I do not. That is work for a human, because it needs an understanding of the world, not just counting. The automated system is great at the boring, repetitive things and terrible at the ones that need judgment. The human is exactly the opposite. It surprises me how rarely this division is taken seriously: people make machines guess direction and then manually watch the stops themselves. With me the roles are handed out the other way around, and the whole philosophy of this EA stands on that.

## One rule at the end

No automatic rule enters the game with me straight from an idea. First the tester and years of data. Then the placebo test: whether a random rule of a similar shape gives the same thing, because if it does, I have discovered noise, not an edge. I write down the conditions for passing before I see the result, so I do not move the goalposts in the middle of the match. Only what passes earns the right to touch money, and even then not on its own.

It is the same setup I built in [D-Logic Capital](https://rdemb.github.io/capital/) on the stock market: a council counts, argues and advises, and the decision is made by a human. On Forex my council is made of silicon and it is called an EA. It advises very well, but it will not make the decision for me.

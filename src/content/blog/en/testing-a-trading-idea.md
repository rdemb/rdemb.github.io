Every strategy idea looks good on a chart. You pick a few nice trades, draw a line, and you already feel you have an edge. The problem is that the market does not pay for nice charts. It pays for something that works on money it has not seen before.

I learned this the hard way, so I built myself a few simple rules to keep from being fooled by my own idea.

## 1. Baseline first, pride later

Before I check whether my idea is good, I look at how something dumb and simple does: "always buy" or "assume tomorrow is like today". If my clever idea does not beat that simple reference point, I do not have a strategy, I have an illusion.

## 2. Costs count from the first second

Spread, commission, slippage. An idea that earns without costs is not a strategy, it is a demo. I add the real cost to every trade, the one I actually pay at my broker. A great many "holy grails" die right here.

## 3. Test on data I have not seen

It is easy to fit the past. The trick is to work on the future. I split the data: I search on one part and check on another, held back. If the edge vanishes on that held-back part, it was a fit to noise, not to the market.

## 4. Is it not just chance?

If you test a hundred ideas, a few will look good by pure luck. So I shuffle the data at random many times and watch how often an "edge" shows up on its own. If my result fits inside what chance produces, it is not a result.

## How it turned out for me

I threw this whole arsenal at my own ideas. And honestly? Most did not survive. My directional edge, on the data I had, evaporated after costs. That is not pleasant news, but it is true, and it saved me real money. One thing held up: predicting how big the move will be, not which way. Less flashy than "I know where the price is going", but that one actually works.

## Why this matters to you

Because this is the whole difference between trading and gambling with a nice interface. It is not about being right. It is about knowing when you are not, and not paying for it out of your own pocket.
